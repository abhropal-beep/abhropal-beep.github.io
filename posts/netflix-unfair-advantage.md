# Why Netflix Has an Unfair Advantage in the Age of AI

*May 2, 2026*

Most Netflix analysis focuses on the same stuff. Subscriber growth, content spend, who's winning between Disney and HBO. That's fine but I think it misses the real story.

My view is pretty simple. Netflix is not a streaming service that competes with other streaming services. It's a technology company that happens to be in entertainment. And that difference matters a lot more than people realize right now.

Also worth remembering what Netflix is actually competing against. People have a few hours a week to kill. They could watch a show, scroll TikTok, play a game, watch sports. The real competitor isn't Max or Disney+, it's whatever else you might do with your evening. Netflix is fighting for all of it.

## The tech thing

Disney, Warner Bros., Paramount, JioCinema. These are all media companies that built streaming apps. They started with content and bolted on tech.

Netflix went the other way. It's an engineering company that got into media. And I think that difference is about to matter a lot because AI is going to change how content gets produced. Costs drop, timelines compress, personalization gets way more sophisticated. The question is which streaming company actually has the engineering bench to build and ship AI systems at production scale. And I think the honest answer is basically just Netflix.

Here's something that surprised me.

I manage part of the security business at Microsoft and one of the products in my portfolio is a secure CDN. I spend a lot of time thinking about global content delivery, edge presence, latency, the operational complexity of running infrastructure at scale.

So I was genuinely surprised when I learned Netflix doesn't use Akamai or Cloudflare or any major CDN provider. They built their own from scratch. Open Connect. Thousands of servers embedded inside ISPs around the world, custom hardware and software, optimized for video throughput, delivering petabytes daily.

Anyone who has worked with CDN technology knows how heavy this is. You don't do this unless you're a real technology company. A media company would just pay Akamai and move on. I remember when I first read about Open Connect I actually went and looked up how many edge locations they have and it's kind of absurd for a company that most people think of as "the app where I watch Stranger Things."

They did all of it because they wanted every last millisecond of performance for the customer. I don't know, it just struck me as a very different kind of company when I saw that.

## The people

Everyone talks about Netflix's culture. The keeper test, radical candor, whatever. But the part that actually matters from an investment perspective is simpler: the company still has the founders' fingerprints all over it, and it still attracts people who are really good at what they do.

I work at Microsoft. Big company, lots of smart people, but I've also seen how much the quality of leadership and the clarity of culture affects what you can actually ship. Strategy is not a slide deck. It's thousands of small decisions made by people who either get it or don't. And when you're at a place where people get it, you feel it. Things just move differently.

Netflix clearly has that. The leadership team today is the same group that built the thing from the start. And you can tell because look at how they've acted recently. When every other streamer was in panic mode about subscriber numbers, Netflix launched an ad tier, killed password sharing, pushed into live events and gaming. All within like two years. I remember watching this from the outside thinking, these guys are just playing a different game. That's not a company run by committees.

When the industry shifts again, and it will, I'd rather bet on the team that moves fast than the one that forms a working group.

## The global thing

This one is maybe the most underrated and honestly probably the hardest to replicate.

Think about Squid Game. It's a Korean show. Made in Korea, for a Korean audience, in Korean. And it became the most watched thing on the planet. Sacred Games is similar. Shot in Mumbai, Bollywood actors, a story that is deeply Indian. And people all over the world watched it.

Who else can do that? HBO can't. Disney can't. JioCinema definitely can't. Regional players don't have global distribution. Hollywood studios don't know how to tell local stories. They'll try but you can always tell when something was made by a committee in LA trying to be "global." Netflix is the only one that can make something in Seoul or Mumbai and have it show up on screens in 190 countries the same day.

And it compounds. The more local content they fund, the more global hits they find, the more they reinvest. Nobody else has this flywheel running.

## So what

I think the market still sees Netflix as a streaming company that's doing well. I see it as something different. The only entertainment company with the tech, the people, and the global reach to own what comes next.
